Katsudon

Katsudon: comfort food and delicious meal, a favorite of not only Japanese but by people around the world.

The origin of this hearty and wonderful dish goes a long way from Tokyo at around 1899. Not much have evolved from the past forms of dish but primarily the dish was served with beef cutlet. It was then during the Meiji Era, when Japan was opening up to western ideas that beef was replaced by pork.

This dish is made mainly by two ingridients: tonkatsu, pork cutlet and donburi, which means large bowl or bowl of rice. This is one of the most popular dishes in Japan both loved by locals and tourists.

Ingridients

  • pork cutlets: 1 thick or 2 thin
  • eggs: 4
  • rice: 1 bowl
  • flour
  • breadcrumbs
  • salt
  • pepper
  • cooking oil
  • onion: 1 whole (thinly sliced)
  • dashi (or chicken stock): 1/3 cup
  • soy sauce: 1 tablespoon
  • mirin: 1 tablespoon
  • sugar: 2 teaspoon

Steps

  1. Making the pork cutlet:
  2. Have a frying pan or cast iron ready: Add a thin layer of oil to the skillet over medium heat.
  3. Season the pounded pork chops with salt and pepper, and dust with a light, even coating of flour.
  4. In one shallow bowl, beat one egg and dip the pork evenly covering it with egg.
  5. Coat the pork with panko.
  6. Place the coated pork chop on the hot oil for 4-5 minutes on each side or until the panko is golden brown
  7. Remove the pork cutlet from the skillet.
  8. Cut the pork at around three quarters of an inch each and let it sit on a paper towel to drain excess oil.

  9. Break the eggs into a bowl and mix until the yolks are broken, but there are still separate areas of white and yolk.

  10. Add the dashi, sugar, soy sauce sake and potato starch to a bowl and stir to combine.

  11. Add the oil and onions to a non-stick frying pan over medium high heat and saute the onions until soft and just starting to brown.

  12. Push the onions to the edge of the pan and add the tonkatsu in the center.

  13. Pour the sauce around the cutlet and cover for 1 minute to let the sauce thicken and the katsu reheat.

  14. Pour the egg all over and around the katsu and sprinkle.

  15. Let it sit for a few minutes but do not let the egg get cooked too much.

  16. Put the hot rice into bowls and cover with the tonkatsu and egg mixture, drizzling any remaining sauce on top.
